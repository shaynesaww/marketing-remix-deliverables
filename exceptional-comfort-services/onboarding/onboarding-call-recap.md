### Q10. A moment where you could've cut a corner — saved yourself time, saved yourself money, nobody would've known — and you didn't. Why not?

**Answer:**

> "That happens all the time."

Steven immediately referenced a call he'd taken yesterday from a customer he got through Benjamin Franklin referral. She needed a TXV (thermostatic expansion valve — the metering device for the refrigerant).

The part wasn't available anywhere. The unit was only about a year old. He spent all day chasing the part down.

Eventually he talked to his Ferguson rep, who connected him to a friend at Gateway, and they got the manufacturer to warranty out a whole coil with TXV — so she didn't have to wait until July to have AC.

The cause of the failure was the previous installer cutting a corner — they didn't purge with nitrogen when brazing the copper lines. Without nitrogen displacing the oxygen, brazing leaves black soot inside the copper.

When refrigerant runs through, it pushes the soot into the TXV screen and jams it. The manufacturer ends up paying for the part even though it wasn't their defect.

Steven was emphatic:

> "That's where you could cut corners every day."

And he doesn't.

He also referenced the maintenance-call practice of finding a piece of uninsulated line set, cutting a piece of Armaflex, taping it on, and not charging the customer.

> "Some companies, they're charging $120 to put Armor Flex on it... I'm going to cut a piece of insulation. I'mma put it there. I'm going to tape it. Then, I'm going to let the homeowner look, look, you've had this pipe dripping for the last such and such amount of time. I took care of it for you today. No charge."

### Q4. What gets you genuinely angry about how HVAC gets done in this industry?

**Answer:**

The laziness, and people not being trained. Not just HVAC — all the trades except construction ("you can't really fake construction").

Plumbing, HVAC, electricians — they go out and know the bare minimum, get the bare minimum training, and the only training the company invests in is sales training. The technical side gets neglected.

Steven argues there should be balance — sales training for guys who struggle talking to customers, fine, but you can't sacrifice the technical depth because the trade keeps growing and equipment keeps changing.

He extended this into a management critique. As former service manager of 30 techs at Carolina, he was the only manager actually holding people accountable — writing techs up, doing spot checks, finding leaves in equipment from yesterday's maintenance, finding unwashed coils.

Other managers let it go, let it go, let it go — then did mass firings of 7–10 people, calling it "reduction of staff" so they couldn't rehire for 90 days in those positions. The cancer is the manager who never held anyone accountable, not the techs who got let go.

He talked through the alternative. If a manager comes to a tech and says:

> "Shane, you're not making your sales on indoor air quality, I need you to do a little better."

That puts something in the tech's head and opens the door for them to ask "Hey, can you teach me?" The big companies don't do that.

They run monthly meetings, put numbers on the board, and publicly shame the bottom while making the top feel cocky and arrogant. The good techs at the bottom leave because no one taught them.

Ten years later all you have is cocky arrogant people who cost you money.

He also called out the licensing loophole in South Carolina — only one license holder per company is required. So companies hire guys with good talk skills, hand them a script, and send them out to charge $345 for a $20–$30 capacitor (plus a $69–$99 service call fee).

He's seen "21, 22 year old kid" techs he personally trained start their own HVAC companies — uninsured, unbonded, unlicensed. It's a combination of greed and incompetence.
