# ECS Ad Recipes

How to write ad copy for ECS without slipping into the high-pressure, fear-based default of HVAC advertising. The point of every ECS ad is to sound like a real person, not a panic salesman.

**Build order for any ad:**
1. Pick the recipe below.
2. Pull the atoms it references.
3. Draft 3 variants (headline + body + CTA).
4. Run anti-AI strip (zero em dashes, no fragments, vary "no obligation" phrasing, no "no upsell" promises).
5. Hand off for image generation (Steven's Higgsfield workflow or Marketing Remix).

---

## Universal ad rules

- **No fear language.** Banned phrases: "your AC is failing," "before it breaks down," "act fast," "limited time," "don't wait."
- **No false urgency.** ECS doesn't run "TODAY ONLY" anything.
- **Steven's voice translates differently to ads than to social.** Tighter. No "look" or "you know." Still warm, still real.
- **Hook → Promise → Proof → Ask.** Every ad fits this four-beat structure.
- **Local always.** Mention the Upstate or a specific town in every ad.
- **One claim per ad.** Don't try to be everything at once.
- **No "no upsell" phrasing.** Show through stories or proof, never promise.

# ECS Email Recipes

How to write any ECS email — sequences, one-offs, follow-ups, newsletters — using the brand KB atoms.

---

## Universal email rules

- **Short.** Most ECS emails should be under 200 words. Steven and Angel are not corporate marketers and shouldn't sound like them.
- **From a real person.** Sign with the actual name. Not "The ECS Team."
- **Plain text feel.** Even if HTML, keep formatting minimal. No big graphics, no fancy buttons. Maybe one CTA link.
- **Subject lines:** under 60 characters, conversational. Not "Limited Time AC Tune-Up Offer!!! 🔥"
- **Anti-AI rules apply.** Zero em dashes. No fragments. No "we're committed to."
- **No fear-based urgency.** "Schedule before your AC fails!" is out. "Your maintenance is due this month — want to set something up?" is in.

### Post 3.1 — Why nitrogen purging matters

**Platforms:** Facebook, LinkedIn

Quick HVAC story so you know what to watch for.

When somebody installs a new AC and they're brazing the copper lines, the right way to do it is to purge with nitrogen. The nitrogen displaces the oxygen, so the inside of the line stays clean.

If they skip the nitrogen (because nobody's watching), the brazing puts black soot inside the line. That soot eventually pushes through and clogs the metering device. Then you're calling a warranty tech to swap parts on a unit that's only a year old.

It's a small step. Nobody checks for it. Most companies don't do it.

We do.

**Sensitivity:** greenlit

2. Did they run gauges and check refrigerant pressure?
3. Did they explain what they found in a way that made sense to you?

If the answer to any of those is no, you're probably not getting a diagnosis. You're getting a sales call.

Get a second opinion. Doesn't have to be us. Just get one.

**Sensitivity:** greenlit

### Post 3.4 — Why your filter matters more than you think

**Platforms:** Facebook, Instagram

Dirty filter is the most common cause of an AC freezing up. Number one. By a mile.

I drove an hour out of my normal service area last year because somebody else had told a homeowner her unit needed a compressor and a TXV. Thousands of dollars.

You know what was actually wrong?

Filters.

Change them every 3 months. Set a phone reminder. Cheapest insurance you'll ever buy on your system.

**Sensitivity:** greenlit

### Post 3.5 — Heat pumps in the Upstate

**Platforms:** Facebook

If you live in the Upstate of South Carolina, you probably have a heat pump.

Heat pumps don't run on natural gas. They run on electricity. They're efficient, but they're sensitive to dirty coils, low refrigerant, and bad airflow.

If your power bill jumped this winter and you have a heat pump, that's usually the first place to look. Either the system is working harder than it needs to, or it's flipping into emergency heat too often (which is expensive electric resistance heat, not heat pump heat).

Get a maintenance check before it costs you a system replacement.

**Sensitivity:** greenlit

---

### Post 3.6 — What "licensed and insured" actually means

**Platforms:** Facebook, LinkedIn

South Carolina HVAC has a licensing loophole most people don't know about.

You only need one licensed contractor per company. The actual techs going out to your house don't have to be licensed at all.

That's how you get 21-year-olds running their own HVAC companies, uninsured, unbonded, charging $500 for a $20 part. They had to pass exactly zero tests to be in your house.

Ask the next HVAC tech who comes to your door whether they're personally licensed. The answer will tell you a lot.

(We're licensed, bonded, and insured. I drove to Columbia myself to pick up the paperwork.)

**Sensitivity:** greenlit (this is the industry-critique post — cap usage at 1/week)

### Post 3.7 — Lake Keowee homes need different HVAC

**Platforms:** Facebook, Instagram

If you own a home on Lake Keowee, your HVAC isn't the same as a regular Upstate house.

The humidity loads are different. The square footage is bigger. You probably have an ERV (energy recovery ventilator), maybe a fresh air intake, possibly a steam humidifier or dedicated dehumidifier. Some homes around the lake are geothermal.

Most of the regular HVAC techs in the Upstate don't see this equipment often enough to know what they're doing with it.

If you're at the lake, ask whoever you're calling whether they've serviced equipment like yours. The answer matters.

**Sensitivity:** greenlit
