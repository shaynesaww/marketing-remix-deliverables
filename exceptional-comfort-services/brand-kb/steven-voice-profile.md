# Steven Tibball — Brand Voice Profile

**Client:** Exceptional Comfort Services (ECS)
**Owner:** Steven Tibball
**Built from:** Onboarding call transcript, May 18, 2026
**Purpose:** Foundation document for writing website copy, social posts, ads, emails, and any other content that needs to sound like Steven. Eventually graduates into a writing skill.

---

## 1. Who Steven Is, In One Paragraph

Steven is a 12-year HVAC veteran from the Upstate who clawed his way up from $10/hr at Target to running his own family business with his wife Angel. He's licensed, bonded, technically sharp, and allergic to the way most big HVAC shops operate. He doesn't pitch. He tells stories. He gets fired up about lazy techs, ripoff pricing, and "21, 22 year old kid" companies popping up uninsured. He pays himself $40k a year so he doesn't have to upcharge. He answers his own phone. He climbs into raw-sewage crawl spaces because the customer needs AC. He's warm without being syrupy, confident without being cocky, and proud of his work without bragging about it. He says "you know," "I mean," and "look" the way most people breathe.

---

## 2. The Core Promise (the felt sense customers should walk away with)

> "I just want them to feel taken care of. I want the quality to be there. I just want them to not have to worry."

Three pillars, in his own words:

1. **Taken care of** — the warm-and-fuzzy feeling. You called the right person.
2. **Quality is there** — it's done right, no shortcuts, technically correct.
3. **You don't have to worry** — no high-pressure sales, no upselling, no surprises, no "your unit needs replacing" scare tactics.

The motto he and Angel wrote on the call:

> **"Family run, friendly feel, and exceptional quality."**

Every piece of copy should be able to point back to one of those three pillars. If it can't, cut it or rewrite it.

## 3. Tone Profile

| Dial | Setting | What it sounds like |
|---|---|---|
| Warmth | High | Genuine, friendly, neighborly. Like he's already met you. |
| Confidence | High | He's been doing this 12 years and it shows, but he never has to say so. |
| Formality | Low | Conversational. Uses contractions. Talks the way an honest tradesman talks. |
| Sales pressure | Zero | No urgency manufacturing. No "act now." No fear. |
| Self-importance | Zero | He pokes fun at himself, admits when he's had to learn the hard way. |
| Technical depth | Moderate | He'll explain a TXV or why nitrogen-purging matters, but in plain English. He never hides behind jargon. |
| Conviction | High when it matters | When he hits on integrity, fairness, or being a real licensed pro vs. a "kid with a tool bag" — he gets pointed. The fire shows up. |

**Net feeling:** like you just had coffee with the owner. Not like you read a brochure.

## 4. Sentence Rhythm and Structure

Steven doesn't speak in fragments and he doesn't speak in essays. His real speech is a mix of medium-length conversational sentences with the occasional short punchy line for emphasis.

### Rhythm rules

- **Mix compound sentences with short hits.** A long, winding "and-then-and-then" sentence, followed by a five-word landing line. That's the cadence.
- **No "Not X. Y." patterns.** That's an AI tell. Write it as one sentence: *"We're not here to sell you a system you don't need — we're here to fix the one you've got."* → instead: *"We're not here to sell you a system you don't need. We're here to fix the one you've got."* Wait — even better, lose the parallel pair entirely: *"If your unit can be fixed, we fix it. If it can't, we tell you straight."*
- **No body-copy fragments.** Headlines can be short. Body should be sentences.
- **Vary line length on the page.** Some lines should breathe; some should land. Don't write four sentences of the same length in a row.

### Example of his actual rhythm

> "I just want them to feel taken care of. I want the quality to be there. I just want them to not have to worry. If they know they're using ECS, they don't have to worry about their system failing. They don't have to worry about high-pressure sales. They don't have to worry about us not being professional."

Notice — three short sentences set up the promise, then three parallel longer sentences spell out what "not worrying" actually means. That's the move.

## 5. Vocabulary — What He Says, What He Doesn't

### Yes — words and phrases that sound like him

- "look," "I mean," "you know," "so," "well" (used sparingly in copy — too many in a row gets sloppy)
- "honest," "fair," "right," "real," "straight," "clean"
- "took care of it," "made it right," "fixed it up"
- "the homeowner," "the customer," "folks," "people"
- "dude," "man" (only in social copy / behind-the-scenes content, never on the website)
- "showed up," "stuck around," "got it handled"
- "I'll tell you straight," "I'll be honest with you," "here's the deal"
- "we'll take care of it," "we'll get it figured out"

### No — corporate / AI / industry-jargon words to strike

- "leverage," "utilize," "solutions," "ecosystem," "synergy"
- "best-in-class," "industry-leading," "world-class"
- "passionate about," "dedicated to providing," "committed to excellence"
- "delight our customers," "exceed expectations" (too sterile)
- "trust the experts," "your trusted partner" (every HVAC site says this)
- "comprehensive," "robust," "seamless"
- "elevate," "unlock," "empower"
- "Don't settle for less" — too aggressive
- Anything that sounds like a brochure from 2008

### Words to handle with care

- **"Integrity"** — Steven himself said everybody throws it around. We can use it, but rarely, and always paired with proof. Better to show it than say it.
- **"Quality"** — fine, but again, prove it.
- **"Professional"** — Steven uses this constantly about himself. Good. But on a website, surround it with what professional actually looks like (uniform, clean truck, answering the phone, no noise in the background).
- **"Licensed, bonded, and insured"** — say it. Steven is proud of it because most of his competitors aren't. This is differentiation, not boilerplate.

## 6. Phrasing Patterns Lifted Directly From Steven

These are real lines from the call. Use them, adapt them, echo their cadence.

**On caring:**
> "I want them to feel warm and fuzzy like I feel warm and fuzzy talking to them."

**On honesty:**

**On the little extra:**

**On free repairs around the holidays:**

**On answering the phone:**
> "Because I'll answer the phone. They're on hold with them."

**On being hands-on:**
> "I'm going to be heavily involved. It's not going to be one of these where I'm going to just disappear."

**On the trade itself:**
> "You have got to be humbled in this career. I've been humbled a couple of times."

**On the work ethic:**
> "I grabbed a hold of all of it and I just absorbed all of it."

When in doubt, go pull a line from this list and let it set the tone for the section.

## 7. Storytelling Over Statements

Steven proves things by telling stories, not by listing claims. Website copy should do the same.

**Weak (claim-based):**
> "We provide compassionate service to homeowners in need."

**Strong (story-based):**
> "Around Christmas, a friend's mom had no heat. Loose wire nuts inside the air handler — a fix that takes 20 minutes if you know what you're looking at. Steven didn't charge her. Told her merry Christmas and asked if she'd leave a Google review. She did."

**Weak:**
> "We go above and beyond for every customer."

**Strong:**
> "An 86-year-old customer had a chandelier coming loose after the roofers banged it. Not an HVAC job. Steven could have left. Instead, he sat in her living room, looked up electricians, found one that didn't also sell HVAC so she wouldn't get upsold, and stayed until that guy showed up."

The website should be built on a backbone of these real moments. The intake call already surfaced 8+ usable stories. They go to work.

## 8. Where the Fire Shows Up

Steven gets pointed — never angry, but plainly direct — when these topics come up:

- Lazy techs who don't get trained
- Companies that only train on sales, not on the trade
- Capacitor markups ($20 part sold for $345)
- "21, 22 year old kid" outfits running uninsured
- Private equity gobbling up local HVAC companies
- Managers who don't hold techs accountable, then do mass firings and call it "reduction of staff"
- Installers who skip nitrogen-purging because nobody's watching
- Salesmen-disguised-as-techs scaring homeowners into new systems they don't need

On the website, this fire can show up — once or twice, never more, never as a rant. The right place is usually somewhere on the About page or in a "Why ECS" section, where it earns its keep by explaining *why* he started his own company.

**Example tone for that section:**

> Most of the big HVAC shops train their guys on one thing: sales. Not the trade. So a homeowner ends up paying $345 for a $20 capacitor, or buying a whole new system when the old one just needed a clean coil and the right amount of refrigerant. That's the model Steven came up under. It's the reason he left, and it's the reason ECS exists.

That's the upper limit. Past that, it tips into preachy.

## 9. What Steven Won't Talk About — and Why It Matters for the Website

He said it directly:

> "We're in the Bible belt here. I don't talk to people about religion. I don't talk to people about politics. I don't talk to people most of the time about sports."

The website follows the same rule. No faith references. No political signaling. No "God bless America" patriotism. Family is fine. Local pride is fine. Hard work and the trade are fine. Beyond that, the website stays focused on the work.

## 10. Voice for Different Surfaces

The voice doesn't change — but the **dial settings** flex by context.

| Surface | Warmth | Length | Slang | Fire |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Homepage hero | High | Tight | None | None |
| About page | High | Long | Light ("look," "you know" used once or twice) | Medium |
| Service page | Medium | Medium | None | None |
| FAQ | High | Medium | None | None |
| Lake home / premium page | Medium-High | Medium | None | None |
| Social caption | High | Short | OK | Medium |
| Email to past customer | High | Short | OK | None |
| Google review reply | High | Short | None | None |
| Ad copy | Medium-High | Tight | None | None |

Rule of thumb: anywhere a homeowner first meets the brand, stay warmer and tighter. Where they go deeper (About, blog, follow-up emails), let more of Steven through.

## 12. Anti-AI Tells to Strip on Every Draft

Per [feedback_anti_ai_application](../../../memory/feedback_anti_ai_application.md):

- **Zero em dashes** in marketing/ad/website copy. Use periods, commas, or rewrite.
- **No "Not X. Y." parallel rhythm.**
- **No body-copy fragments** (headlines OK).
- **Vary "no obligation" / "free estimate" phrasing** — don't make it the same line every time.
- **Drop "no upsell" promises** — show it through stories, don't promise it as a slogan.
- **No "warm and fuzzy" as a tagline** — it's a quote from Steven about feeling, not a brand line. Quote him saying it; don't paste it on a banner.

---

## 13. Quick Test for Any Draft

Before sending a draft, ask:

1. **Could Steven say this out loud on a service call without sounding fake?** If no, rewrite.
2. **Is there a story or a concrete example backing the claim?** If no, add one or cut the claim.
3. **Does the rhythm vary, or is it all the same sentence length?** If flat, sharpen one or two lines.
4. **Are any AI-tells present** (em dashes, parallel fragments, generic adjectives)? Strip them.
5. **Would a homeowner in Pickens read this and feel like they've already met him?** That's the bar.
