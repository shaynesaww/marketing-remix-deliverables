## The kind of tech ECS hires

Honest. Comfortable talking to people. High integrity. Shows up on time, in uniform, ready to walk into someone's house and represent the brand. Clean cut. Not pulling on his work shirt in the driveway five minutes before the appointment.

Steven calls it "the whole package." It's the technician who can hold a real conversation with an 80-year-old at her kitchen table, then go diagnose the unit correctly, and then explain in plain English what's wrong without making her feel talked down to.

## The kind of tech ECS won't hire

Cocky. Arrogant. Know-it-all. The car-salesman type who walks into a homeowner's house already calculating his commission. Steven has seen plenty of them. He's managed plenty of them.

The hard truth about that profile — they usually know enough about the trade to be convincing. But they bring habits picked up at the big shops, and those habits don't unlearn easy. The script for selling fear instead of fixing the unit. The reflex to quote a system when a part would do. The instinct to make the customer feel like she got a deal when she actually got a bill.

> "You've got to undo whatever nonsense they've got going on first."

A tech like that often costs more than he brings in, because the customers he closes are the customers who never call again.

## Humility is the prerequisite

Steven says it plainly.

> "You have got to be humbled in this career. I've been humbled a couple of times."

He's shocked himself. He's almost fallen out of attics. He's fallen off ladders. He's hung from attic hatches when the ceiling wouldn't hold him. That's the trade. Electrical mistakes start fires. Refrigerant mistakes burn skin. A misstep on a roof is a hospital trip.

A tech who hasn't been humbled yet is dangerous to himself and dangerous to the homeowner. Steven won't put a guy in a truck before he's ready, and he won't let his own son skip steps either. The older boy has talked about being a manager at 22. Steven's answer is that he'll get there, but not by jumping the line.

## The team vision

ECS stays family-run, with quality people hired alongside. No subcontractors. No franchise expansion. No selling to private equity in five years. Steven stays in the trucks, double-checking the work, training the new techs the right way, and keeping the standard tight enough that it can grow without going soft.

## The principle

Steven doesn't think everybody in HVAC has to be making a killing. He thinks the price should scale with the actual work, not with what the customer can be talked into paying. That belief shapes the whole company.

> "I just don't feel like everybody's got to be making a killing. Like you got to scale the amount of money that you're making."

## How he keeps prices honest

Steven pays himself $40,000 a year right now. That's less than somebody pulls in working a gas station. Angel still works her full-time job and brings in her own income, which gives them room to keep ECS overhead low while the business grows.

Low owner pay plus low overhead means he doesn't have to mark a part up six times to keep the lights on. A capacitor is a good example. The part costs about $20. Steven can sell it installed for $199 and still make money. The big shops in town are charging $345 for the same job because they have to feed a whole building, a fleet of trucks, a dispatcher, a sales manager, and a commission pool.

> "If I can sell it for $199 and make money, why are you selling it for $345?"

## On full system replacements

The most expensive system Steven has sold to date is around $10,000 to $11,000 for a 4-ton install. A comparable big-shop quote for the same equipment, in the same house, is closer to $18,000. Same coil, same condenser, same line set, same warranty. The gap is overhead and commission.

## Why ECS won't pay techs on commission

Steven won't pay a service tech $35 to $40 an hour plus 8% commission on every job. That's the exact formula that turns a tech into a salesman. A $1,600 system suddenly gets quoted at $3,000 or more, and the homeowner has no way to tell the difference. The tech makes his bonus. The shop hits its number. The customer pays a year's worth of vacations for somebody they've never met.

ECS pays differently and hires differently because of it.

## First-year reality check

Most HVAC companies lose money their first year. ECS is on track to clear $25,000 to $35,000 in profit in year one. That's not because they're hustling people. It's because the overhead is small enough that fair pricing actually works as a business model.

If you call ECS for a part, you're going to pay what the part costs to install and a fair margin on top. That's it.

## Nitrogen purging when brazing copper

Every install at ECS gets nitrogen-purged when the copper line set is brazed. That sounds like an inside-baseball detail, and it is, but here's why it matters to the homeowner.

When you braze copper without nitrogen flowing through the line, the heat reacts with oxygen inside the pipe and leaves a film of black soot on the inner wall. Once refrigerant starts running, that soot gets pushed through the system and lodges in the TXV screen, which is the metering device that controls how much refrigerant feeds the coil. A clogged TXV starves the coil, ices it up, and eventually kills the system.

Steven has seen units less than a year old fail this way. The manufacturer ends up replacing the coil under warranty even though the defect wasn't theirs. The original installer cut a 15-minute corner and the customer pays for it later in lost cooling and a service call.

> "That's where you could cut corners every day. And I don't."

Most installers skip nitrogen because nobody's standing over them watching. ECS doesn't skip it.

## Diagnose first, replace second

Steven has driven an hour out of his service area to look at a unit that two other companies said needed a new TXV and a new compressor. The actual problem was a dirty filter. He didn't sell the part. He cleaned the filter, charged the customer for the trip, and told her how to keep it from happening again.

The rule at ECS is simple. Find what's actually wrong before you quote a fix. If a $20 part solves it, it's a $20 part with a fair install charge. If it really does need a system, that conversation gets had honestly, with numbers that hold up.

## Real licensing, not the loophole

South Carolina has a license-holder loophole. The law only requires one licensed contractor per company. So a big shop can put their license on the wall, then send out a truck driven by somebody who's never been licensed, never been bonded, and never been trained past the sales script.

Steven holds the contractor's license himself. He drove to Columbia personally to pick it up because he didn't want to wait on the mail. He's bonded and insured. He has a bachelor's in HVAC. He's a certified Generac dealer for service and installation. The work that goes out under the ECS name has a licensed contractor's signature behind it.

That's the standard. It doesn't move.
