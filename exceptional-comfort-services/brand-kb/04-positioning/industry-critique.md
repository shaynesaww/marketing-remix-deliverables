## Usage cap — read this before you write from this atom

This is the "fire" material. Steven gets pointed when these topics come up, and that energy is part of the brand. But it tips into preachy fast.

**Rules for any copywriter pulling from this atom:**

1. **Max one usage per website surface.** If the homepage already uses the capacitor-markup angle, the About page can't use it again. Pick the right place once.
2. **One industry-hot-take social post per week, max.** Across both Facebook and Instagram combined.
3. **Always end with what ECS does instead.** Never leave the reader sitting in the negative.
4. **Pointed, not preachy.** No rants. No villainizing individuals or named companies (per guardrails, no naming former employers).
5. **Story over claim.** Use the specific example, not the broad accusation.

## The four fire topics, with the ECS counter for each

### 1. Lazy training

The big shops train their techs on one thing — sales. Closing. Objection handling. How to walk a homeowner through the financing app on a tablet. The technical side gets the minimum the law allows, because the company makes its money on what gets sold in the driveway, not on what gets diagnosed under the house.

**What ECS does instead:** Steven came up technical first. The diagnosis happens before the quote, and the quote reflects what the unit actually needs.

### 2. Managers who never hold anyone accountable, then do mass firings

Steven was the only service manager out of his peer group who actually wrote techs up, did spot checks, and pulled leaves out of coils that should've been cleaned the day before. The other managers let things slide for months, then did "reductions in staff" of seven to ten people at a time, blocking the position for 90 days. The techs got blamed. The managers stayed.

**What ECS does instead:** Steven runs his own service. The accountability is the owner.

### 3. The 21- and 22-year-old "kid" companies

Steven has trained guys at the big shops who turned around and started their own HVAC companies before they could legally hold a contractor's license. Uninsured. Unbonded. Driving an unmarked truck. Good talkers, light on the trade, and one mistake away from a fire in somebody's attic.

**What ECS does instead:** Licensed, bonded, and insured. Steven's contractor's license is on file in Columbia. Twelve years in the trade before the LLC paperwork ever got filed.

### 4. The capacitor markup

A capacitor is a $20 part. The big shops charge $345 installed, plus a $69 to $99 service-call fee. The tech is on commission. The manager wants the average ticket up. The customer pays for both.

**What ECS does instead:** ECS sells the same capacitor installed for $199 and makes a fair margin. Steven pays himself $40,000 a year so he doesn't have to mark anything up six times to keep the doors open.

## Where this atom is safe to deploy

- Once on the About page, as the "why he left and started ECS" beat.
- Occasionally as a story-led blog seed (one fire topic per post).
- Sparingly in social, paired with a customer story showing the ECS way.

Past those uses, save it. The brand is the work, not the critique.
