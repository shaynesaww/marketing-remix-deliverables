## The job he refused to put his name on

Years back in Myrtle Beach, Steven got dispatched to a service call on a unit that was less than six months old. Brand new install from a different company. The customer had been waiting outside in the summer heat all day, and by the time Steven pulled in she was belligerent drunk.

He went out to the condenser and started looking the install over. It wasn't just sloppy. It was a fire hazard. Wires run in a way that was going to cook, connections that shouldn't have passed any inspection, the kind of work that puts a house at risk. He's not going to name the company that installed it, but he wasn't going to clean up after them either.

He came back around to the front and told her straight.

> "Look, you need to call the company that installed it and you need to have them make this right. I'm not going to touch that and put my name on it."

She didn't take it well. She came after him so hard her own neighbor had to walk across the street and physically hold her back so Steven could get to his truck. A few days later she left him a one-star review.

## What the story shows

The right call isn't always the call that ends with a happy customer and a good review. Steven walked away from a job that would have made the company money because putting his name on a fire hazard was the wrong trade. He'd make the same call again tomorrow.
