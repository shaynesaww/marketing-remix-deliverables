## The diagnosis that didn't add up

A buddy of Steven's, somebody he used to work with at the big company, called him up worried about his mom. She lived in Belton, about an hour out of the area Steven normally runs calls. Her unit was freezing up, and the tech who'd been out there had told the family it needed a TXV and a new compressor.

Steven heard that and stopped him.

> "There is no way that it needs both of those. If it needs a compressor, then it wouldn't be freezing up because a compressor wouldn't be able to compress anything. It doesn't make sense."

That's the kind of diagnosis that only makes sense if you're trying to sell a job. A frozen coil means refrigerant is still moving through the system, just not the way it should. If the compressor were actually shot, there'd be no pressure to freeze anything in the first place.

Steven drove out there anyway. An hour each way, on his own time, into territory he doesn't even service.

He pulled the filter rack open and looked. The filters were caked. They hadn't been changed in a long time. That was the entire problem. Restricted airflow across the indoor coil dropped the coil temperature below freezing, and the moisture in the air kept building up ice. He swapped the filters, let the system thaw, and it ran fine.

## What the story shows

A misdiagnosis like that costs a homeowner thousands of dollars. The technical knowledge isn't optional in this trade, and neither is the willingness to drive an hour out of your way to keep somebody from getting fleeced.

## Two companies, thousands of dollars, wrong fix

Janice went to middle school with Angel. She found Steven through Angel's Facebook after two of the bigger HVAC outfits in the Easley area had already been to her house. Between them they'd charged her thousands of dollars to replace her coil.

Her unit was still acting up. The compressor kept shutting down on her, especially during the hottest part of the day, and neither company could tell her why.

When Steven got there and put his gauges on it, the answer was sitting right in front of him. Whoever had changed the coil had overcharged the unit with refrigerant. Too much refrigerant means too much pressure, which means the compressor runs hot, which means it trips its internal overload and shuts itself off to keep from burning up. That's not a coil problem. That's an install problem.

He pulled the extra charge out, got the pressures back where they belonged, and the unit ran fine. The fix took a fraction of what she'd already paid the other two companies for.

## What the story shows

She didn't need a new coil. She didn't need a new compressor. She needed a tech who actually read the gauges instead of selling the next biggest part. That's the gap between a sales-trained tech and a technically trained one, and it costs homeowners real money.

## What he does on a maintenance call when nobody is watching

When Steven is out doing a routine maintenance, he checks the lineset. The lineset is the copper that carries refrigerant between the outdoor unit and the indoor coil. The big line is supposed to be wrapped in foam insulation called Armaflex. Bare copper sweats, drips onto whatever is underneath it, and runs the system harder than it needs to run.

He sees it all the time. The insulation never got installed, or a piece of it tore off, or the sun rotted it out. Most companies will write it up as a recommended repair and quote the homeowner around a hundred and twenty dollars to fix it next visit. The actual fix takes about three minutes and costs a few bucks in material.

Steven keeps Armaflex on the truck. He cuts a piece, slips it over the bare section, tapes it down, and then walks the homeowner over to show them.

> "I took care of it for you today. No charge."

## What the story shows

A small fix on a maintenance call is the easiest place in this trade to mark something up, because the homeowner doesn't know what it should cost. Steven uses the same moment to do the opposite. The piece of insulation costs him almost nothing, and it tells the homeowner exactly what kind of company they just hired.
