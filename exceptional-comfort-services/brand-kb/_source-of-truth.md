## Service area

The Upstate of South Carolina. Primary towns:

- Pickens
- Easley
- Greer
- Seneca
- Greenville
- Surrounding Oconee County and Pickens County
- **Lake Keowee** — yes (the Sunset / lake-home market). **Lake Jocassee** — no, too far.

## The ideal customer

Steven said it directly on the call: the house doesn't matter to him. A house is a house. What matters is **the conversation**.

His sweet spot:

- Homeowners who can hold a normal, down-to-earth conversation
- Middle-aged and older (skews better than younger — communication is more reliable)
- People who care about quality and don't need to be sold
- People who refer their friends, family, and neighbors
- Sam-the-plumber-type customers: quality-focused themselves, who only refer people they'd trust in their own house

## The premium tier — Lake Keowee

Lake Keowee customers have high-end second homes (one of Steven's customers has a $5.5M home there). Their expectations are different:

- Shoe covers on
- Trucks that don't leak oil ("their driveways probably cost more than my house")
- Presentation matters more
- Different equipment in play: fresh-air intakes, ERVs, dehumidifiers, steam humidifiers, sometimes geothermal

ECS serves geothermal **for maintenance and service**. Not for new installation (that gets reserved language on the website). But Steven will take a geothermal service call gladly — the word-of-mouth from that customer base is worth it.

## Customers ECS doesn't enjoy serving

Steven was honest about this:

- Belligerent or unsafe customers — see the Myrtle Beach walkaway story
- Customers who treat the trade like a commodity haggle
- Very low-income jobs — not because of the people (Steven's heartstrings get pulled and he ends up taking care of them anyway), but because they're financially painful for the business. Acknowledged but not advertised.

## Don't target on the website

- Apartment maintenance contracts (commercial-only operation isn't ECS's lane)
- Whole-house generator installations (no plumbing/electrical license — covered in services/generator-service.md)
- Anyone outside the listed service area

Every draft passes through these guardrails before publishing.

## 1. Don't name former employers

Steven explicitly asked that his former employers **not** be named on the website. Angel asked him to confirm this the night before the call. References are fine — *"a 30-tech crew at one of the bigger players in town"* — but no:

- Carolina Heating Service
- One Hour Heating & Air
- Benjamin Franklin Plumbing (Angel's current employer)
- East Coast Air (his cousin's Myrtle Beach company)

In social posts and on the website, treat them as "the big shops," "a regional player," or just "the company I came up at."

## 2. No religion, politics, or sports

Steven's own rule:

> "We're in the Bible belt here. I don't talk to people about religion. I don't talk to people about politics. I don't talk to people most of the time about sports."

The website follows that rule. No faith references, no political signaling, no "God bless America" patriotism, no team logos in copy. Family, local pride, hard work, and the trade itself — all good. Anything that picks a side — not on ECS surfaces.

## 3. The "fire" topics are capped

Steven gets pointed when he talks about lazy techs, ripoff capacitors, mass firings, and the licensing loophole. That energy is part of the brand. But:

- **Maximum one usage of the industry-critique angle per website surface.** Pick the right place (usually the About page or a "Why ECS" block).
- **One industry-hot-take post per week** on social, max.
- **Always end with what ECS does instead** — never leave the reader sitting in the negative.
- **Pointed, not preachy.** No rants. No villainizing individuals.

## 4. No high-pressure sales language

This is a core differentiator. Strip these patterns out of every draft:

- "Act now," "Don't wait," "Limited time"
- "Your system is failing" (the exact line Steven hates)
- "You need to replace this" (unless Steven would actually say it about that unit)
- "Schedule today before it's too late"
- Fear-based scarcity language of any kind

## 5. Don't promise "no upsell"

Per Shayne's anti-AI memory: drop the phrase "no upsell" or "no upselling" as a promise. Show it through stories instead (the 86-year-old chandelier story, the filter-not-compressor story). Customers feel manipulated when they read "we promise not to upsell you." Let the work speak.

## 6. No claim language we can't back

- "Best in town" — no.
- "Most trusted" — no.
- "#1 rated" — only if there's a real ranking we can cite, and even then, sparingly.

## 7. AI tells to strip on every pass

Per the [voice profile](../steven-voice-profile.md) and the anti-ai-application memory:

- Zero em dashes in marketing/ad/website copy
- No "Not X. Y." parallel rhythm
- No body-copy fragments (headlines OK)
- Vary "no obligation" / "free estimate" phrasing — never the same line twice
- No "warm and fuzzy" as a tagline (it's Steven's quote, not a brand line)
- Strip "leverage," "utilize," "solutions," "comprehensive," "robust," "seamless," "elevate," "unlock," "empower"

## Who ECS is

Exceptional Comfort Services is a family-run heating and air company in the Upstate of South Carolina. Steven and Angel Tibball started it on October 29, after Steven spent 12 years working his way up the trade — installer, service tech, then service manager over a 30-tech crew at one of the bigger players in town. He saw how the industry actually gets run from the inside, and he didn't like a lot of what he saw. ECS is his answer.

## The motto

> **Family run, friendly feel, and exceptional quality.**

Steven and Angel wrote that down together. It's the brand in one line.

## The three pillars (what customers should feel)

1. **Taken care of.** The warm-and-fuzzy feeling that you called the right person. Steven and Angel answer the phone. They show up clean, in uniform, in a clean truck. They treat your home like it matters.

2. **Quality is there.** The work is done right. Real licensing, proper diagnosis, no shortcuts, no skipped steps. If a corner can be cut, ECS doesn't cut it.

## The name

"Exceptional" is in the name on purpose. Steven pointed out on the call that every HVAC company throws around "integrity" — you see it on everybody's trucks. ECS put their standard in the name itself. It's the bar they have to clear every day.

## What ECS is not

Not a franchise. Not private-equity-owned. Not a sales operation pretending to be a service company. Not going to grow into something different.

## The setup

Then the company got bought out. He was making $250,000 a year and was miserable.

They talked for a couple of months about Steven's vision — what he wanted to build, how he wanted to work, and most importantly: working alongside Angel again. Steven loved working with her at his cousin's company years earlier; she'd been the office manager there.

The new owner made it sound like Steven could eventually buy the franchise. For about six months it was great. The owner asked his opinion on things. Then everything changed.

## The breaking point

The owner brought in a numbers guy from the franchise's board. The guy knocked Steven back into a service truck, stopped listening to him, and was losing the company $100,000 a month. Steven wasn't making 40 hours a week and was going into debt.

Around early October, Steven confronted the owner.

> "Dude, something's got to happen. I'm going into debt."

The owner said: *"Well, you're not good with your money."*

Steven wanted to hit him. He didn't. He went home and talked to Angel.

She said: *"Maybe it's time. You just need to do something."*

## Building ECS from zero

They filed the LLC on October 29.

Steven did it the hard way and the right way at once — contractor's license, business license, bond, insurance, all of it. He drove from Greenville to Columbia himself to pick up the contractor's license rather than wait on the mail. No attorneys. He learned the business side as he went, mostly with ChatGPT as his accidental co-founder.

> "I haven't talked to nobody. I've been chat-gpting it up. I've been learning this stuff as I go. We took it by the horns."

He named the company Exceptional Comfort Services on purpose. He'd had the name in mind for years, even back in Myrtle Beach. The logo was the last piece — and as he put it on the call, the early version "looked like ass" until ChatGPT helped him design a real one.

Within roughly six months, ECS hit $150,000 in revenue. Angel was still working her day job at Benjamin Franklin and helping with paperwork in the field after hours and on weekends.

## What the story tells you about ECS

Steven didn't start the company because he wanted to be a boss. He started it because the way the trade gets run inside the big shops wasn't acceptable to him. Every decision since has been about not becoming what he left.

## What ECS does

ECS replaces aging or failed air conditioning systems and installs new equipment for additions, remodels, and new builds. Single systems and multi-system homes. Steven sizes the equipment properly, pulls the right permits, and installs to the standards the trade actually calls for, which includes nitrogen-purging the line set during brazing so the system doesn't end up with soot jamming the TXV six months later.

## Who it's for

Homeowners whose existing system has reached the end of its useful life, who are remodeling, or who are putting in a new system from scratch. The customer who wants a real conversation about what they need, not a salesman in a tech uniform pitching the largest system on the truck.

## How Steven approaches it

Steven won't recommend a replacement unless the unit actually needs it. Many of the calls he runs end with a repair, not a quote on new equipment. When a replacement is the right answer, the price reflects the real cost of doing the job correctly, not the margin a big shop needs to feed a sales-commission structure.

For reference, the most expensive system Steven has sold to date is around $10,000 to $11,000 for a four-ton install. The same system from one of the bigger regional players runs closer to $18,000. That's not because ECS is cutting corners. It's because Steven pays himself modestly and doesn't have to pad every invoice to cover overhead and commissions.

The nitrogen-purging discipline matters here. Steven told a story on the call about a one-year-old unit where the previous installer skipped that step. Black soot from the brazing migrated through the refrigerant and jammed the metering device. That kind of failure is invisible at install and shows up later. ECS doesn't skip it.

## What ECS won't do or claim

No upselling on system size that the home doesn't need. No quoting a replacement when the existing unit can be repaired. No corner-cutting on install standards because nobody is watching the truck.

## Proof points and quotables

- Most expensive install to date around $10K to $11K for a four-ton, vs. roughly $18K from the bigger shops
- Nitrogen-purging on every brazed line set
- Licensed, bonded, and insured
- 12 years in the trade, including install crew time at the start of Steven's career

Generator work is one of the areas where doing the job right and respecting the licensing rules matter at the same time. ## What ECS won't do or claim

ECS does not advertise or perform new generator installation as a standard offering. ECS is not licensed in South Carolina for the plumbing or electrical scopes that a new install legally requires. Any claim or implication otherwise gets cut from copy on sight.

## Nitrogen purging when brazing copper

Every install at ECS gets nitrogen-purged when the copper line set is brazed. That sounds like an inside-baseball detail, and it is, but here's why it matters to the homeowner.

When you braze copper without nitrogen flowing through the line, the heat reacts with oxygen inside the pipe and leaves a film of black soot on the inner wall. Once refrigerant starts running, that soot gets pushed through the system and lodges in the TXV screen, which is the metering device that controls how much refrigerant feeds the coil. A clogged TXV starves the coil, ices it up, and eventually kills the system.

Steven has seen units less than a year old fail this way. The manufacturer ends up replacing the coil under warranty even though the defect wasn't theirs. The original installer cut a 15-minute corner and the customer pays for it later in lost cooling and a service call.

> "That's where you could cut corners every day. And I don't."

Most installers skip nitrogen because nobody's standing over them watching. ECS doesn't skip it.

## Diagnose first, replace second

Steven has driven an hour out of his service area to look at a unit that two other companies said needed a new TXV and a new compressor. The actual problem was a dirty filter. He didn't sell the part. He cleaned the filter, charged the customer for the trip, and told her how to keep it from happening again.

The rule at ECS is simple. Find what's actually wrong before you quote a fix. If a $20 part solves it, it's a $20 part with a fair install charge. If it really does need a system, that conversation gets had honestly, with numbers that hold up.

## Real licensing, not the loophole

South Carolina has a license-holder loophole. The law only requires one licensed contractor per company. So a big shop can put their license on the wall, then send out a truck driven by somebody who's never been licensed, never been bonded, and never been trained past the sales script.

### Post 3.1 — Why nitrogen purging matters

**Platforms:** Facebook, LinkedIn

Quick HVAC story so you know what to watch for.

When somebody installs a new AC and they're brazing the copper lines, the right way to do it is to purge with nitrogen. The nitrogen displaces the oxygen, so the inside of the line stays clean.

If they skip the nitrogen (because nobody's watching), the brazing puts black soot inside the line. That soot eventually pushes through and clogs the metering device. Then you're calling a warranty tech to swap parts on a unit that's only a year old.

It's a small step. Nobody checks for it. Most companies don't do it.

We do.

### Post 3.5 — Heat pumps in the Upstate

**Platforms:** Facebook

If you live in the Upstate of South Carolina, you probably have a heat pump.

Heat pumps don't run on natural gas. They run on electricity. They're efficient, but they're sensitive to dirty coils, low refrigerant, and bad airflow.

If your power bill jumped this winter and you have a heat pump, that's usually the first place to look. Either the system is working harder than it needs to, or it's flipping into emergency heat too often (which is expensive electric resistance heat, not heat pump heat).

Get a maintenance check before it costs you a system replacement.

**Sensitivity:** greenlit

### Post 3.6 — What "licensed and insured" actually means

**Platforms:** Facebook, LinkedIn

South Carolina HVAC has a licensing loophole most people don't know about.

You only need one licensed contractor per company. The actual techs going out to your house don't have to be licensed at all.

That's how you get 21-year-olds running their own HVAC companies, uninsured, unbonded, charging $500 for a $20 part. They had to pass exactly zero tests to be in your house.

Ask the next HVAC tech who comes to your door whether they're personally licensed. The answer will tell you a lot.

(We're licensed, bonded, and insured. I drove to Columbia myself to pick up the paperwork.)

If somebody quotes you $345 to replace a capacitor on your AC, hang up.

### Post 5.3 — When techs only get sales training

You know what most big HVAC shops train their techs on? Sales.

Not the trade. Not how to diagnose. Not how to actually fix what's wrong with your unit.

How to sell.

That's why you keep getting the "your system is failing, you need a new one" speech. Not because the system is failing. Because that's what they were taught to say.

We train on the trade first. The sales conversation takes care of itself when you actually know what you're talking about.

### Post 5.4 — Pay-and-bench, then mass firings

I used to manage 30 techs for a big HVAC company. Here's something most homeowners don't see:

The cancer isn't the techs who got let go. It's the manager who never held anybody accountable.

ECS is built so that doesn't happen. Small team. Held to a standard. Coached if they're behind.
