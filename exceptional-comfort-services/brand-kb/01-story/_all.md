## The setup

After Steven's first long stretch at his cousin's company in Myrtle Beach (about four and a half years), he came back to Greenville and spent almost eight years at a big regional HVAC company. He worked his way from bottom service tech to service manager of a 30-tech crew.

Then the company got bought out. He was making $250,000 a year and was miserable.

A guy from another big shop reached out. They talked for a couple of months about Steven's vision — what he wanted to build, how he wanted to work, and most importantly: working alongside Angel again. Steven loved working with her at his cousin's company years earlier; she'd been the office manager there.

The new owner made it sound like Steven could eventually buy the franchise. For about six months it was great. The owner asked his opinion on things. Then everything changed.

## The breaking point

The owner brought in a numbers guy from the franchise's board. The guy knocked Steven back into a service truck, stopped listening to him, and was losing the company $100,000 a month. Steven wasn't making 40 hours a week and was going into debt.

Around early October, Steven confronted the owner.

> "Dude, something's got to happen. I'm going into debt."

The owner said: *"Well, you're not good with your money."*

Steven wanted to hit him. He didn't. He went home and talked to Angel.

She said: *"Maybe it's time. You just need to do something."*

## Building ECS from zero

They filed the LLC on October 29.

Steven did it the hard way and the right way at once — contractor's license, business license, bond, insurance, all of it. He drove from Greenville to Columbia himself to pick up the contractor's license rather than wait on the mail. No attorneys. He learned the business side as he went, mostly with ChatGPT as his accidental co-founder.

> "I haven't talked to nobody. I've been chat-gpting it up. I've been learning this stuff as I go. We took it by the horns."

He named the company Exceptional Comfort Services on purpose. He'd had the name in mind for years, even back in Myrtle Beach. The logo was the last piece — and as he put it on the call, the early version "looked like ass" until ChatGPT helped him design a real one.

Within roughly six months, ECS hit $150,000 in revenue. Angel was still working her day job at Benjamin Franklin and helping with paperwork in the field after hours and on weekends.

## What the story tells you about ECS

Steven didn't start the company because he wanted to be a boss. He started it because the way the trade gets run inside the big shops wasn't acceptable to him. Every decision since has been about not becoming what he left.

## Where he came from

Steven was born in New York and grew up in Fort Lauderdale, Florida, raised by a single mom. When his mom remarried, the family bounced between Myrtle Beach and Greenville a few times. Steven landed in Greenville around 16. When his mom decided to move back to Myrtle Beach again, he told her he was done moving. He stayed. She left. All three of his kids were born in Greenville. He considers himself from the Upstate at this point — about 25 years.

## How he got into the trade

Steven worked on cars from age 14 with his stepdad, who owns an auto repair business. HVAC came later, almost by accident. He was making $10 an hour as a back-room supervisor at Target when his cousin in Myrtle Beach — who owned an HVAC company called East Coast Air — posted on Facebook looking for help.

Steven messaged him joking: *"Yo, sign me up."*

His cousin told him if he was serious, come ride along for a day. Steven and Angel drove down to Myrtle Beach, stayed a couple of days, and Steven rode with him for one. By the end of that day, he knew.

> "Dude, this is definitely for me."

## The decision to change everything

Steven and Angel weren't in a good place financially. Credit in the 500s. Living in a mobile home. Angel was waitressing. They decided to make a hard pivot.

They let the house get foreclosed on. Steven backed a U-Haul up to the front porch, kicked the railing off, carried everything straight out, and drove away. They rented a townhouse in Myrtle Beach and started over.

He started as an installer. About a year and a half in, he was learning service while still installing. He moved up to field service manager fast — *"I grabbed a hold of all of it and I just absorbed all of it."*

In his first year running a service truck by himself, Steven closed almost a million dollars in sales.

## What the story tells you about him

He doesn't wait around. He doesn't make excuses. When he decides something has to change, he does the hard version of changing it.
