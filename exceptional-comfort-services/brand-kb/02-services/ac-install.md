## What ECS does

ECS replaces aging or failed air conditioning systems and installs new equipment for additions, remodels, and new builds. Single systems and multi-system homes. Steven sizes the equipment properly, pulls the right permits, and installs to the standards the trade actually calls for, which includes nitrogen-purging the line set during brazing so the system doesn't end up with soot jamming the TXV six months later.

## Who it's for

Homeowners whose existing system has reached the end of its useful life, who are remodeling, or who are putting in a new system from scratch. The customer who wants a real conversation about what they need, not a salesman in a tech uniform pitching the largest system on the truck.

## How Steven approaches it

Steven won't recommend a replacement unless the unit actually needs it. Many of the calls he runs end with a repair, not a quote on new equipment. When a replacement is the right answer, the price reflects the real cost of doing the job correctly, not the margin a big shop needs to feed a sales-commission structure.

For reference, the most expensive system Steven has sold to date is around $10,000 to $11,000 for a four-ton install. The same system from one of the bigger regional players runs closer to $18,000. That's not because ECS is cutting corners. It's because Steven pays himself modestly and doesn't have to pad every invoice to cover overhead and commissions.

The nitrogen-purging discipline matters here. Steven told a story on the call about a one-year-old unit where the previous installer skipped that step. Black soot from the brazing migrated through the refrigerant and jammed the metering device. That kind of failure is invisible at install and shows up later. ECS doesn't skip it.

## What ECS won't do or claim

No upselling on system size that the home doesn't need. No quoting a replacement when the existing unit can be repaired. No corner-cutting on install standards because nobody is watching the truck.
